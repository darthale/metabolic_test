Potential Energy Reserves and Glycogen Storage Based on Segmental Skeletal Muscle Mass SMM:
While oxygen tolerance and Oxidative Stress Index OS-I can be used to determine the maximum "safe" training times, the analysis of the Segmental Skeletal Muscle Mass SMM serves to calculate the energy reserves actually available in the form of intramuscular glycogen stores. These are calculated based on the potential amount of glycogen that can be stored in the muscles for their movement. Since this value is also individual, the overview is created using a spectrum of 10 to 25 g/kg body weight, noting that the maximum amount of 25 g/kg is rarely realistic and must be specifically trained.
These come into play when planning nutrition during training and can also help create a nutrition strategy for competitions. Additionally, the relationship between SMM to VT1 and VO2Max provides good insight into whether the training is constructive and goal-oriented or whether destructive, catabolic processes are in progress that need to be interrupted.
Breakdown of Segmental Skeletal Muscle Mass SMM:
For the calculation of glycogen stores, the segmental skeletal muscle mass SMM responsible for movement is divided into three sections (upper body, core, and legs), which are relevant for movement in different proportions.

Load Distribution in Movement:
Since glycogen metabolism can only occur in active muscles on a muscular basis (glycogen molecules are too large to be moved from cell to cell), the percentage shares of SMM in movement are relevant for calculating the loading time.
There are also individual aspects that factor in (mobility and adaptive range of motion); for an overview, general segment proportions are shown here:

The difference between individual sports and triathlon is primarily that on the bike, a quieter, less active upper body is assumed due to the aerodynamic position. This results in a more active core when running, as it is already pre-fatigued.
